# My Story
![img](pages\about_me\profile_pic.jpg)

I’ve taken a somewhat untraditional path to geospatial. The majority of my years after graduating from college were focused on brewing beer commercially and outdoor guiding. I was intensely passionate about both, and even to this day, I look back fondly on that period of my life.


Unfortunately, the two even combined couldn’t pay the bills. For this reason, I started looking elsewhere for a more stable and financially viable career path. I graduated with a degree in Economics from Fort Lewis College which was a satisfying learning experience in its own right albeit any serious desire to pursue a career in finance. My true interest in the degree was the analytical side which I found an affinity for. 

I stumbled upon GIS one day after literally googling “analytics and the outdoors”. I was surprised I never heard about this GIS-thing, and was even more surprised that many of my friends through college used it during some of their courses. I instantly was drawn into it and began realizing how it reflected many of my inherent interests – that is “analytics and the outdoors”. Suffice it to say, I felt like I found something that was both deeply stimulating on a personal level and something that could actually pay the bills...this was the fall of 2018.

Two months after making this decision, I was able to get a small internship for an even smaller non-profit called the 1947 Partition Archive. They worked to archive oral stories of India’s terrible and bloody Partition of 1947. I worked as a GIS analyst that maintained a web map that showcased the geographical location of families and individuals the organization had interviewed, including their place of origin and where they had been forced to migrate. At the time, they had a laborious workflow for publishing that involved manually validating each person’s location info. I was given free rein to improve this system and I did so by integrating a Python script that automatically validated locations and corrected coordinates based on a standard for each known location. I am deeply grateful for this opportunity as it was my first step toward a new career but it also gave me a chance to make a huge improvement in their daily operations.

In January 2020, I enrolled in Penn State’s GIS Certificate with the intention of continuing my education in their Masters of GIS program. This was the beginning of a two-and-a-half-year journey developing traditional geographical knowledge, utilizing contemporary GIS applications, and learning technical skills applicable to the geospatial field. I decided to lean my degree heavily on programming in Python and imagery analytics. My final capstone project developed a solution to reclassify shadows in aerial imagery using Python and an object-based classification approach.  

Nine months after beginning my formal education, I received an opportunity to work for the National Park Service in Page, AZ. Glen Canyon National Recreation Area is governed by the NPS, and it is there where I worked for 10 months as a GIS assistant. My work there was deeply enriching which included things outside of GIS. I was able to take a boat up the Colorado River and help remediate cultural artifacts as well as explore and learn more about the desert ecosystem and its fragility. This is also where I developed a keen interest in environmental conservation and ways that GIS could help protect these beautiful areas. All my work culminated in a vegetation analysis using imagery analytics to determine high-risk vegetation types throughout the Park. The analysis helped inform cultural management of erosion risks in heritage sites as well as ecological efforts to restore areas suffering from desertification. 

Proceeding my time in Page, with just enough time to move back to Colorado, I stepped into a new internship working for CoreLogic. This was an opportunity that allowed me to live in beautiful Boulder, CO, and begin a career with a company building data products for the real estate industry. The internship allowed me to utilize remote sensing data to highlight some of the key improvements in our new Digital Elevation Model products (DEMs). The analysis improved the validation of these products and helped set us apart from other competitors. 

Working for CoreLogic has been a rewarding opportunity to work on many different products yet continue to develop my Python skills. Shortly after my internship, I was given a full-time role as a GIS analyst for the company. I was hired primarily to utilize my Python experience and automate an old geoprocessing build of one of our data products. Within the first 6-months, I was able to build an entirely new process that enabled a user to automatically validate the data throughout the process and ensure that the final delivery was fidelitous.

I now currently work as a Data Engineer for CoreLogic spearheading the development of a new flood product that will be delivered later this year. Again, another great opportunity for me to continue developing my programming skills but more importantly, this project gave me invaluable experience in product design. Speaking with domain experts and translating their specifications into highly performant code has been the biggest learning lesson from this project.

As I continue along this path of spatial engineering, I hope to be given many more of the opportunities that I have listed so far, as I believe all of them have played an integral role in where I am now. 

So, if you’ve made it this far in my journey, congratulations, you now know a little bit about me!

But, you say, “Austin, that was all about boring work stuff – what do you like to do *outside* of work??” Well, I have many interests outside of work. One interest that I have been completely smitten with is rock climbing. Along with this and more recently, exploring the world on two wheels in the form of bikepacking has been, for me, the definition of fun.

I've done a handful of thru-hikes which include twice the Colorado Trail and one time the High Sierra Route (which I dearly hope I have a chance to do again in my lifetime). 

Currently, I’m learning Spanish, how to make (good) sourdough, and hanging out with my beloved cat, Miso. 

If all that still wasn’t enough and you want to learn more, feel free to reach out via any social media platform or drop an email. I’d be happy to talk and maybe learn a little bit about you!
